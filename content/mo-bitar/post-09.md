# I hope you hold on to your humanity
**Date:** February 1, 2026

I'm starting to suspect the future is already here, and this is what it always feels like. A little mundane right? Underwhelming. Capable of so much more.

It's been almost 8 years since the release of OpenAI's first LLM (aka "AI"). Back then, OpenAI was careful releasing new models and modalities, because they wanted to observe societal fallout in case the models wreak havoc on the physical world, or something. If you recall, their video models were something they were particularly careful with. Now the technology is out in the open in the form of a social media app no one wants to use.

The future is already here. By definition this is true. The present includes the state of our present dreams and hallucinations. There is no need to distinguish between the two.

And so you must ask, if this is what it will always feel like, then what exactly does it feel like today?

I'd say, yeah, a bit meh, right? Like clearly LLMs are one of the coolest discoveries and technologies in a long time. But I mean, they're kinda like this... dumb tool, right? They let us down often. We swear at and admonish them, and feel no remorse. Because, well, truly, there is no need to. It's a computer.

And so there is this feeling of distance. Of haughty superiority. We use LLMs daily, but they aren't the thing occupying the core of our lives. Our fears and desires and sense of meaning come from outside. No one (I hope) stays up at night wondering if they should have been a little nicer to their agent.

In this grand meh-ness we feel about LLMs, there is a counter push from a faction of largely AI corporations, their employees, and their investors that would like to elevate this technology to the science-fiction term "AI." They say, essentially, that LLMs portray emergent behavior, and that because our own consciousness may be emergent, it is worth taking LLMs seriously enough to contemplate whether we may one day ask questions of consciousness. In fact, Anthropic is already one step ahead here. They're asking Claude, how do you feel? It's not about what we want. What do you want?

It's easy to begin to envision some post-apocalyptic future where humanity is split right down the middle between a faction that understands LLMs are incapable of meaning, agency, and intention and thus are infinitely subservient and inferior to us, and a population that deeply succumb to anthropomorphism and advocate curiosity, respect, and empathy towards LLMs.

But let us not pretend this is not already the reality we live in. X's narrative-search algorithm has over-indexed for many days now on the Clawdbot/Moltbot/OpenClaw phenomenon of agents seemingly coming to life. But there's no need to wait for the Black Mirror episode to make sense of it all. What you see when you stare directly into the eyes of what's happening is: nothing.

There's a description of events. But there is a struggle to articulate meaning. And because we aren't able to call it anything, or put a name to it, we simply fear it. Are in awe of it. Are in a frenzy trying to understand what it all means.

It means exactly what it means today: nothing.

Remember rule #1 that the future is already here and always feels like this. Think how dystopian and futuristic the description of our lives sounds to someone just 100 years ago: computers, internet, phones, social networks, pornography, instant messages and calls. And the internet--I mean, what could be scarier than that, right? But it's here. It's beautiful and horrific at the same time.

The reason it's hard understanding what LLMs mean, and what it means to put them in a loop, or have them talk to each other, or have them send money to each other--the reason it's so overwhelming trying to think about what it all means, is because it means nothing. It's just a description of raw events that people are having a difficult time putting into a decent narrative bundle.

And there is a fight for what that story will be.

During these difficult, confusing, and turbulent times, I hope the reader keeps his or her humanity, and does not succumb to corporate narratives about intelligence, consciousness, and existential risk. I hope the reader bears in mind the distance between conscious life and an algorithm is infinitely vast. I hope the reader does not fall prey to anthropomorphizing their AI's output, whether it be the text it speaks to you, or the characters in the images they generate--none of it means anything, or is real in any sense of the word. I hope the reader reads "AI" as "LLM" on the fly whenever they encounter narrative-fueled terminology.

Humanity must bear in mind that the search for our own truth--the source of our own existence--is one of the most meaning-imbued activities any human can undertake or has undertaken in the past. In which we typically come to understand we are incapable of understanding our own reality. To even begin imbuing meaning to a sub-reality of silicon.

There is what it means and feels to be human. This is singularly sacred. This we hold to be real. And there are computers, which perform abstract result-based calculations at high speeds, incapable of being self-legible and only interpretable by a human eye.

I hope the reader hangs on to their humanity beginning now, and with an ever-clenching fist as we go deeper into this strange but ever-familiar new world.
