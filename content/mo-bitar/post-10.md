# There is no skill in AI coding
**Date:** January 28, 2026

Andrej Karpathy recently shared observations about AI coding that resonate with many developers. His key insight: current AI models consistently produce junior-level code regardless of user expertise.

## The Problem with AI Code

According to Karpathy, AI mistakes have shifted from simple syntax errors to subtle conceptual problems. Models make unfounded assumptions and proceed without verification. They struggle with confusion management, rarely seek clarification, and avoid pushing back on questionable requests.

The code quality issues extend beyond logic. AI tends to over-engineer solutions, creating bloated abstractions and unnecessarily complex implementations. When confronted with alternatives, the systems readily acknowledge better approaches but don't arrive at them independently.

## The Simon Analogy

Mo Bitar extends this observation through a metaphor: imagine hiring a developer named Simon who promises transformation but delivers problematic code. While executives celebrate his speed, engineering teams recognize the technical debt accumulating.

Simon's defining characteristics include:
- Making assumptions without verification
- Accepting every proposal without team consultation
- Overcomplicating solutions
- Occasionally deleting or modifying code outside the assigned scope

Despite explicit guidance and feedback, Simon's fundamental approach remains unchanged. Yet firing him proves impossible--he's too productive and management loves his velocity.

## The Takeaway

The reality is striking: skill variations don't exist in AI coding. Beginners and experienced engineers receive identical outputs. This limitation persists even with detailed specifications or lengthy instructions.

Human oversight remains essential, not because users lack expertise, but because the technology itself has inherent constraints that no amount of user sophistication can overcome.
